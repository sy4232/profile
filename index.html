Mitigating urban heat risk across scales

My doctoral research builds a multi-scale account of urban heat — from the ecophysiology of a single urban forest to citywide patterns of risk and inequity — by coupling physics-based simulation, machine learning, and Earth observation with in-situ measurement.

Urban-forest ecophysiology & microclimate

A transient multiphysics CFD framework with evapotranspiration models of increasing physical fidelity, validated against in-situ microclimate and infrared surface-temperature data. A stomatal-resistance model shows cooling that extends ~300 m downwind and exceeds 1 K at pedestrian height.

CFD · Evapotranspiration · HPC

High-resolution climate-risk analytics

A weather-aware land-surface-temperature model at 10 m / 10 min resolution (R² > 0.96), fusing urban morphology and surface cover from Landsat and Sentinel-2 with diurnal signals from GOES-16, validated against ECOSTRESS — cutting the cost of citywide heat analysis.

Machine learning · Remote sensing · Downscaling

Cooling centers & the net benefit of heat refuge

A net-benefit analysis of urban cooling centers that weighs the relief from at-home heat exposure against the additional exposure incurred travelling to and from the center. By accounting for the full door-to-door heat budget, the framework identifies for whom, and under what siting, a center actually reduces net heat burden.

Cooling centers · Heat exposure · Net benefit

Research agenda

Looking ahead, I aim to build an integrated, decision-relevant science of urban heat: coupling multi-scale models so that leaf-level physics informs citywide projection; acquiring meteorological data at high spatiotemporal resolution; and driving down the computational cost of climate-risk analysis so that equitable adaptation can be planned, not just described. My background spanning engineering, environmental policy, and a decade of regulatory decision-making positions this work to move from quantification to actionable planning for cities facing intensifying heat.
